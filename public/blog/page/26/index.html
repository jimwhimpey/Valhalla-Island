I understand the desire to shower in the morning. It’s a nice, fresh way to wake yourself and start the day. I don’t understand only showering in the morning.

The day is when you get dirty. Bringing that dirt to bed then washing it off in the morning, just in time to get dirty again doesn’t make sense. The bed is clean, if you want to be clean the next day showering only at night is works because you don’t get dirty while you sleep.

If you hold water preservation goals and limit yourself to a single shower per day, the evening option has to be the way to go. Sleep clean, start the day clean.

A Universal IE6 Stylesheet →

It’s crazy to still be talking about IE6 but the reality of the problems it causes remain.

The idea that a website has to look identical in every browser, even at the expense of design in browsers that render a page correctly, is insane. No one but the developer looks at the same website in multiple browsers, if it looks different between them, no one knows (let alone cares).

I’ve always said that the content, the reason the user is at the website in the first place will always be available in IE6 whether it’s with display perfection, CSS bugs or no CSS at all. It’s not worth hours hacking your way to perfect cross-browser consistency in a browser that is fundamentally flawed when no IE6 user knows what it’s supposed to look like anyway.

With anything under my control the philosophy is to completely ignore IE6. I spend my time developing better websites for browsers that work and worst case scenario (the most common scenario) it doesn’t look “right” in IE6, big fucking deal.

The universal IE6 stylesheet is doing a little more than nothing and I think, gets the balance right.

Tagging Input Standard

I’m frustrated by inconsistent tag input standards. There’s two kinds:

tag, tag one, another, a third tag

tag "tag one" another "a third tag"

The majority of applications use the former and it’s the best format. It never forces you to consider the amount of words in a tag and the comma is a natural separator.

Delicious uses the latter, an application for which tagging is extremely useful and whose experience most benefits by reduced data input friction.

Spaces are quick when entering single word tags but don’t work as a logical separator, it feels like I’m writing a sentence rather than a series of individual keywords. It’s most annoying when entering multi word tags having to think about and account for multiple word tags before input. It stifles the spew of tags that come to mind when you’re quickly record and move on.

The problem is exacerbated when almost everything else does it differently making it difficult to even get used to the inferior system.

Let’s decide on one method (the first) and make it the standard.

Marketing Dollar

Daniel Jalkut of Red Sweater Software in response to Cabel Sasser of Panic’s post on their expansion to 13 staff from two man beginnings:

It struck me while reading Cabel’s post, for instance, that Panic apparently has no marketing staff. And yet they’re one of the best known brands in indie Mac software. Paradox? Is there something about Panic’s product and staff that does its own marketing? Or would they be an even bigger success had they hired a marketing person from the start? Smile On My Mac, by contrast, consists of just three people, one of whom is completely dedicated to marketing. Is this a waste of revenue or a brilliant component of their plan for world text expansion domination?

I’m a believer in the maxim: make a great product and the marketing will take care of itself. A fantastic press release, email newsletter or advertising campaign, no matter how brilliant, can’t make a mediocre application popular.

Everyone who’s ever worked at Panic has taken care of the marketing indirectly by building something great and genuinely useful.

Hiring a marketing person is pre-internet thinking. It made sense when making someone not in your immediate geographical area aware your product existed was a serious hurdle. Now everyone has global reach at their fingertips and word of mouth is literally multiplied by the million.

Is it a paradox that Panic, with their 13 staff, none of which are marketing, have a website that feels more down to earth, friendly and useful despite Smile on my Mac’s much smaller team of 3 that includes one fully dedicated to marketing?
