The Truly Silent Phone

I don’t like the concept of phones. It shouldn’t be socially acceptable to interrupt anyone at any time with a demand for their complete attention. That said, situations exist where information is important enough to warrant interruption.

To partly deal with it I rarely had my phone set to anything but silent. In reality “silent” mode with vibration on is actually quite loud and jarring, much more jarring than many ringtones and especially if your phone is sitting on a hard surface. Even in a pocket I find the vibration more of a shock than a vibration-less ringtone.

It’s only recently that I identified anxiety attached my ringing and vibrating phone and the attention it demanded. So I decided that as well as permanent silent mode I’d turn off vibrate and I haven’t looked back, it’s a joy.

If my phone is in view the most notification I get of a call or text is a lit up screen. Much less distracting and more easily ignored than a loud ring or vibrate. If it’s not in view I get no notification at all. Even when in view I get zero notification of new email, an added bonus.

Sure I miss a lot of calls but in a couple of months it’s never mattered, people leave a message and can wait until I next check my phone and I’m free to call. If I’m expecting a call and my phone isn’t within peripheral vision I’ll temporarily turn off silent mode.

It’s a simple change and it may sound dramatic but it feels liberating.

The ‘Book’ is Dead

The “Book” is Dead

Mark Pilgram on David Flanagan having to get a salaried job after 15 years living on book sale royalties and the problem of piracy.

I couldn’t agree more with the comments Pilgrim quotes. So few people have an inherent desire to pirate and it’s an accessible option to even less. iTunes “beat” piracy by being better. iTunes is easier, faster and more reliable, it’s simply a more desirable option even to those with the technical acumen to pirate content. With the “bonus” of being the ethical option.

As commenter Peter points out:

I have been an OReilly Safari subscriber for several years. I can recommend this to every developer out there. … Yet, must admit it still pains me that for ~$500/year we as honest subscribers can not get the same convenience (offline access, unencumbered PDF’s) as people who just download a pirated PDF library for free.

Piracy doesn’t “win” for books because it’s free but because it’s better.

Fancy Hands →

For $35/month Fancy Hands, “Personal Assistant for Everyone”, can do 15 tasks of this nature (taken from their 10 most commonly requested tasks:

  • Restaurant Reservations
  • Scheduling a car service / taxi pickup
  • Schedule a haircut with [stylist] on Friday after 1pm
  • Find a couple upholsterer options near where I work
  • Call some hotel and extend my stay for three nights instead of two

Seems to me that you’re paying $35/month to send 15 emails instead of making 15 phone calls or doing 15 Google searches. Typing “Upholsterer near X location” into Google Maps is initially faster than sending a coherent email and not only will the results be returned instantly but they’re interactive. As for booking a taxi, a haircut or changing a hotel reservation these are 30 second phone calls which resolve instantly.

Legitimately time saving and useful stuff like picking up laundry, recurring tasks or even the graphical theme of their website — a bellhop carrying bags — they can’t do. I imagine the real advantage of a personal assistant would come from tight integration with your life. Things like the ability to go places for you, fill out your calendar, read your email and buy things are all outside the realm of Fancy Hands.

Maybe people are willing to pay $35/month for the illusion of a personal assistant.
