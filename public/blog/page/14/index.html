More Thoughts on the Nissan Leaf

The car rather than the website this time. Starting with the facts:

  • It gets 100 miles per charge.
  • Charging requires a home installed charging station.

The site fails to mention how long it takes to recharge which makes me assume it comes no where near rivalling a 10 minute petrol fill up. I’m guessing it requires an overnight charge. So even if there were public recharge stations and you want to be able to get home, the leaf’s effective range is 50 miles.

There is one scenario to which cars are perfectly suited: long distance travel to remote locations. Places to which operating public transport is not and will never be feasible or sensible. Short range electric cars like the Leaf are competitors to public transport, car sharing schemes, walking and bicycling within cities.

Cars in cities, electric or otherwise, are the problem. If you live in a sufficiently densely populated area where any conceivable destination is within 50 miles public transport is feasible and should be the solution.

It reminds me of “urban transport devices” like the Segway and the YikeBike. They’re competing with walking and riding a bike. There’s no problem they’re solving.

Darkroom →

Years ago I read about the elegance and simplicity of Ruby on Rails and couldn’t wait to try it out but each of the few times I tried to dive in the following year I was overwhelmed by convention I didn’t understand and abandoned the idea.

When recently in Sydney I told toolmantim about my failed attempts using Rails and he told me about Sinatra, the lightweight Ruby web framework which sounded like what I had originally hoped Rails would be.

Just two weeks ago I started Darkroom in my spare time with no experience writing Ruby. Today it’s basically complete: a big Flickr slideshow presentation!

See it in action with this gorgeous set of nature photos by Wolfgang Bartelme or Jorge Quinteros’s photostream.

There’s even a bookmarklet for redirecting Flickr URLs to Darkroom URLs!

The idea was born from my frustration with Flickr’s 500px wide default with no way to navigate between photos and anything larger outside the Flash slideshow. I Hardly Know Her tackled the same issue but falls short, defaulting to a smaller size and not offering titles or descriptions.

Sinatra has built in support for HAML and SASS two more tools I’ve wanted to try for a while. They’re both a joy. Writing vanilla HTML and CSS feels like running in soft sand. I’m planning to convert almost every web property I have to Sinatra if only for the effortless HAML and SASS support.

The last piece of the puzzle is deployment with Heroku, a process which is nothing short of magic. From start to finish the combination of Sinatra, HAML, SASS and Heroku is the kind of elegance and simplicity that I’d always hoped Ruby on Rails was.

My Photo Processing Workflow

  1. Take a lot of photos.
  2. Import everything into Aperture.
  3. Browse all the photos, 5 star the ones I like, delete the clearly terrible.
  4. Filter by 5 stars, choose the best of similar photos and zero star the others. Zero star anything that, on second thought, isn’t great.
  5. With the remaining 5 stared photos: crop if required, auto colour levels, adjust exposure, contrast, saturation and recovery if necessary.
  6. Fill out title and caption metadata on remaining photos.
  7. Upload to Flickr as a set using Flickr Uploader for Aperture (Aperture 3’s built-in Flickr Uploader sucks).

There is a high end restaurant that sits literally over the river not far from where I live. I have no idea how it got the position, there’s no other business anywhere near the bank of the river 5km in either direction. They’re quite famous in Brisbane for their location and apparently the food is pretty good, albeit expensive.

They recently attached a platform jetty off the main restaurant building, filled it with tables and opened it as a fish and chip shop. This pleased me because there’s no other fish and chip shop within walking distance and I liked the idea walking along the river before eating on the water.

I picked up a menu next time I passed, found that the cheapest item available was $15 and wrote off the idea forever.

It sits right on the bikeway that runs into the city and I pass this place often. From the bikeway you can see the every table on the jetty. In the three months or so it’s been open I have seen one person eating there.

One month into the opening they must have got sick of putting out and putting away a full set of tables for next to no customers so they started putting out only three tables. This meant only about 25% of the jetty was used and it looked ridiculous. Still no one ate there.

A month later I guess they realised how pathetic it looked to have a jetty with only three unoccupied tables and they switched back to the full compliment of unoccupied tables. Since then I still don’t see anyone eating there.
