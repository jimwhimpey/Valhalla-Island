Layers

The graphic in my last post was composed using Acorn. I always download these alternative graphics applications, spend about five seconds drawing a line or a box, quit and never open them again, hardly a thorough trial. As simple as the result, this was first time practically trialling any of them.

My biggest issue with Fireworks and Illustrator is bloat. I use a tiny subset of Fireworks’ features. Which would be fine if everything I don’t use wasn’t in the way, cluttering the UI, making the entire application slow and prone to crashing while bugs remain in the subset I do use.

Acorn solves that issue, it launches in a second with a well-thought out, organised UI offering only the features you need.

Yet, like other hopeful Creative Suite challengers, it persists with the confusing, unintuitive and plain frustrating layers metaphor. Far and away the best thing about Fireworks and Illustrator is that they treat elements as individual objects, use layers if you want but they’re purely an organisational tool.

Photoshop has by far the most frustrating implementation of layers, you can’t even select a box without consulting the layers palette. Acorn isn’t that bad but in my small trial I was frustrated twice. First by the inability to shift select elements between layers and then by having the opacity meter affect every object on the layer of my selected object rather than simply my selected object.

I want to draw elements, resize them, align them and set type — I don’t want to administrate layers. While apps like Acorn hold on to the archaic and unintuitive layers paradigm they’ll never be a viable option for me.

Business Idea

A service that allows products to be bought from US online stores, shipped to a US address before being forwarded on to an international address. Charge face value for shipping and a low percentage of the item’s value or a flat fee (say 3% or $20) for each shipment.

The Apple Store is a great example of where I’d use this service all the time.

Cost of the baseline Mac Pro from the Australian Apple Store: AU$4500

Cost of the baseline Mac Pro from the US Apple Store after exchange rate conversion: AU$2860

Over AU$1600 cheaper for an identical computer that simply originates at a different location, even if shipping was another $100 and the US-based package forwarder charged $100 for their simple service, it’s still much cheaper than buying it from the Australian store.

The biggest issue would be trust. What’s stopping them from keeping whatever you need forwarded? Apart from the “never deal with x again” blog post that could mean they never receive anything again. By then they might have a nice new computer delivered to their door so who cares?

If a service could build up a solid reputation of trustworthiness starting with inconsequential, cheaper items, it might work.

Buying a Couch

I’m more than happy with the build and simplicity of Ikea furniture for almost anything but when it comes to a couch, I want something big, robust and super comfortable. Since moving we’ve now got everything we need and it’s time to start replacing our crappiest things that have simply done the job with some better things. I’ve always wanted a corner lounge and our current couches are old hand-me-downs.

We quickly found a great corner lounge for $2300, much too expensive.

A couple of weeks later we resumed the search and found the exact same couch on sale for $1600! A massive $700 off and well within our price range, great! We measured our lounge room and went to the furniture store, measuring tape in hand, ready to lay down the cash.

We found the couch, sat in the couch, loved the couch and then measured the couch. Too big, no way it’d fit. We browsed the other 80+ couches in the store, none came anywhere near the comfort, practicality (it has a built-in bed) or price of the original brilliant lounge.

We came home and slumped into our ridiculously low, vinyl, old, two seater lounges, dejected.

Then I had a brilliant idea, a complete rearrangement of the lounge room into a layout so crazy it just might work. The more we thought about, the more we measured and mapped out, the more possible and just plain superior to our current setup it looked! Great! We can buy the couch, first thing next weekend!

This afternoon I check the website looking for a phone number so I could ask about getting it up our stairs and into the apartment. Horror! The couch is no longer on sale for $1600 but for $2000! Fuck!

I call the store, begging for the sale price of just two days ago, when we were ready to buy. He told me the probably-bullshit story of their suppliers upping the price but agreed to meet me half way, $1800. I reluctantly accepted.

Tumblarity is a curse. My tumblarity graphed would look something like a sine wave. I start low, feel no pressure, post what’s on my mind when it’s on my mind and find myself on a roll. My tumblarity rises. I feel a burden to keep it high, get anxious about finding or creating content to post, when I do have something I rush it together then worry about the right time of day to post or whether it’s good enough to post at all. The result is contrived posts and less of them. My tumblarity gradually (it actually happens pretty quickly) drops to rock bottom, I feel free again, the writing and creativity flows. Repeat.

This whole process may have always happened naturally with slow weeks, good days and the like. Whatever algorithm powers tumblarity just does a great job of noticing.
