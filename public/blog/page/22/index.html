Ideal Online Ordering

I order a lot of things online. Even a bad online ordering experience usually beats out the actually going to a store equivalent. I feel like I’ve run the entire gamut of ordering systems and there’s a sweet spot. Apple’s online store nails it. This is what they do that makes it so good:

  • A store for each country or area with prices in the local currency and accurate delivery estimates. Everything in the store is available to you. For an Australian, this is where Amazon falls down.
  • Free shipping on everything, no exceptions.
  • The fastest shipping available, no exceptions. You’re not forced to choose between a multitude of slightly different options, they don’t use shipping to cut costs, you simply get the fastest. I’ve had multiple situations where I’ve ordered something from the Apple online store at 3pm one day and had it arrive before 9am the next.
  • Two emails: the first one sent out immediately to confirm your order the next usually a few hours later confirming it’s been sent out that includes a link to the package tracking facility. I’ve had packages arrive from Ebay extremely quickly but without even a confirmation that they received my payment. Equally bad is an email every time your order is glanced at sideways.

Rehabilitation Observation

Situations are always interesting when a random sample of people are brought together and forced to interact. My rehabilitation program is a great example: eight people, half male, half female, discrete age groups, social status, referred from different places and working in a variety of fields.

Something that bothers me is people who complain about problems they can fix but choose not to solve for whatever reason. This kind of person is represented heavily in my group.

I’m not the typical patient in my program. My back problems have existed for 5 weeks, since breaking my bones, most others have chronic problems that have been around for years or even decades. Invariably back problems can be solved simply — with the right exercise, body mechanics, stretching and posture.

Some group members have been attending back and neck rehabilitation programs for years, temporarily solving their problems but relapsing when they get “too busy” to keep up their exercise and stretching. It’s expected that we’ll continue our floor and cardio exercises for at least an hour a day five times per week when the program is complete. There’s no one that hasn’t complained about the burden of having to exercise when they have so little time as it is while simultaneously complaining about back pain, the stress it causes and the things it prevents them doing.

The patient who seems to have the most day to day issues flat out refuses to give up wearing high heeled shoes despite the physio and occupational therapist’s dire warnings against them. Another just doesn’t like wearing shoes with good arch support. It blows my mind that these people are willing to enrol in a two week, full-time rehabilitation program but are totally unwilling to wear a different kind of shoe. I can’t imagine how frustrating it must be for doctors.

It’s not limited to taking care and responsibility for their own well-being either. A consistent topic of conversation is how terrible the traffic is. I talk about how great it is living in the city, so close to the hospital and all other amenities. They turn their nose up, “I could never live in the city”. Meanwhile, they’re spending two hours a day stuck in stop start traffic, complaining bitterly about it and can’t find a single hour per day to look after their health.

The exercise physiologist asked me whether I’ll have the time to continue the floor exercise program after the two weeks. Of course I’ll have time because I rent an apartment very close to my workplace, I work sane hours and getting back to 100% health is the most important thing in my life. Without that, little else, including the type of shoe I wear, matters.

Minimalism Versus Simplicity

Often friends or family upon seeing my bedroom or computer setup comment on the “minimalism”. That description has never sat well with me. It’s not until I’ve started following minimalism blogs like Minima and Minimal Mac that I’ve realised why.

What I’m striving for isn’t minimalism but simplicity. While simplicity is taking away all but the essentials I feel like minimalism is taking away all but the essentials and then taking away more merely for the sake of less at the cost of utility, convenience or comfort.

The Ride to Cure Diabetes

Early next year I’m taking part in the Ride to Cure Diabetes. It’s a 160km ride through the hills of the Barossa Valley in South Australia. To be eligible to participate I need to raise $3500. I’ve setup a website to collect donations, chart my progress, explain what Juvenile Diabetes is, why I’m doing it and why you should donate.

My sister was diagnosed with Type 1 Diabetes when she was 12 years old and I’ve seen first hand how it can affect a young person’s life. Type 1 Diabetes is treatable but it’s a heavy burden for a child to hold when the complications can be so severe. The Juvenile Diabetes Research Foundation, the charity behind the ride to cure, are solely dedicated to finding a cure for Type 1 Diabetes. They don’t waste money on “awareness raising”, every donation goes towards the research that will eventually find a cure.

While the main purpose of riding is to raise funds for JDRF, recovering from my bike accident a little over a month ago is secondary. 160km through hilly terrain is an intimidating thought while my bike is crushed and 18 of my bones remain broken but it’s a goal that will only aid my recovery.

Donations are handled through my page at JDRF but you can find more information and a cool graph I’ll be continually updating at my own fundraising website. I’ll be hugely grateful for donations of any amount. If you run a business and would like a sponsorship arrangement I’m open to anything, contact me at jimwhimpey@me.com. Thank you!

Radtech’s Full Screen Noise Filter

I prefer a glossy screen over matte for the rich colour and super brightness. If it’s up to me, which it usually is, I’m not computing in a brightly lit area so glare and reflections are not an issue. At work however it’s not up to me, the bright fluorescent lighting is unavoidable and when using a glossy screen there’s glare and reflections.

To combat the glare at work I bought a ClearCal Anti-Glare film from Radtech and it arrived today. I spent 5 minutes going through the (expectedly) painful process of applying it to the screen, left it on for five minutes, then peeled it off and threw it in the bin.

It no doubt killed the glare but with it was a fine layer of distortion or noise covering the screen making everything slightly blurry and making text difficult to read. A co-worker likened it to removing the anti-aliasing from everything on the screen. It looked terrible and gave me a headache. I tried to tell myself to just live with it for a day or two and allow myself to potentially warm to it but I couldn’t, it had to go.

The one positive to come from it was that as far as screen problems go, now the glare doesn’t seem so bad.

§ Aug 3rd, 2009

Improving by the Day

It’s hard to believe it’s been a month since my accident. It still feels like yesterday I was breathing the stifling air of the Princess Alexandra Hospital.

My recovery has been rapid and feels like it accelerates every day. I’m moving less cautiously and more freely, getting stronger and feeling less tired all the time.

Both my orthopaedic and maxillo facial outpatients appointments have gone well. The doctors are happy with my progress.

I’ve been back at work this past week, not full time but close to it. I handled it well except for Tuesday when I got extremely tired early and was useless for half the day. My boss has been giving me a ride to work each morning but I had the stamina to walk home the last three afternoons. Both being at work and walking home has done the world of good.

The only situation where my back continues to give me any pain is after lying down for hours. I can sleep for a couple of hours totally pain free but any longer than that and it starts to get more than uncomfortable. The only thing that can relieve it is standing or sitting up. I was getting up 3 or 4 times a night but lately it’s down to the once and the pain is low enough to sleep through most of the time. A week ago I’d be dying to get out of bed in the morning to relieve the pressure but now I’m able to hang around for a while. The difference sleeping better makes is huge.

My jaw is gradually getting stronger. I’m eating food with a little more substance now. I can eat a whole banana normally and can bite through a piece of chocolate if I suck on it for a little while. The more I work it the stronger it gets but I won’t be anywhere near eating properly until the bones are fully healed in another two and a half weeks.

My teeth still feel a little strange and I’m sure once the wire is taken out of my mouth in a bit over week I’m going to lose at least one tooth but I’ll deal with that then.

I’m hitting the scars across my face and neck with chlorsig twice a day without fail and it’s paying dividends. I still have no feeling at all down my nose and around the centre of my mouth. I can feel the nerves rebuilding themselves through the extreme itchiness that no scratching can appease. It seems feeling is creeping back into those areas.

In another week I start a back and neck rehabilitation program at the Wesley Hospital, just a block and a half from my apartment. It’s a thorough and intensive two week, full time program involving physiotherapists, occupational therapists, neurologists and psychiatrists. It’s supposed to be the best program available for rehabilitating my kind of injuries and is very expensive but thankfully covered by insurance.

I’m getting to the point where basic movement is no longer a problem and I’m itching to do more. Right up until the day of the accident I was running, riding or swimming at least an hour a half a day. That’s been followed by a month of almost complete inactivity. I’m looking forward to the rehab program because it’ll get me moving again with the medical supervision to make sure I don’t do any further damage to myself.

At the end of those two weeks my bones will be healed, I’ll be physically active and if not back to 100% strength I’ll be well on my way to complete recovery.

It’s great to see Lance back and take a spot on the podium but as a whole this year’s tour has not been satisfying. Cycling’s most ungrateful and plainly disrespectful child took the yellow while Cadel Evans once again languished behind a less than supportive, let alone strong team.
