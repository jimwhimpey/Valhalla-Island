GPS Cycling Tracking Software Options Review

Before I got my Garmin Edge 705 I was using the iPhone + RunKeeper combo. RunKeeper was for simply tracking the distances I was riding before I had a bike computer rather than for any kind of analysis. I get much more data out of the Garmin and it’s lead me head first into a category of software I hadn’t previously explored.

The options are numerous both online and client side but each have their own sets of positives, negatives and quirks. I’ll attempt to summarise my findings over the last week.

RunKeeper

The website presents the relatively small amount of data RunKeeper collects well. Every other app here has features that stretch beyond what RunKeeper’s can provide. If all you have is an iPhone this is without a doubt the way to go.

Positives

  • Great graph and map integration. Hover over the graph and it shows you the point on the map.
  • Great looking, readable graphs.
  • Good presentation of summary data like total distance, average speed, etc.
  • No need to upload anything, taken care of by the iPhone app.
  • Nice simple date based method of managing rides.
  • Being web based, easy to share.

Negatives

  • Inflexible. Small map that you can’t make bigger without hiding the graph.
  • Bad map presentation, in Map view it’s difficult to make out the path.
  • You have to use an iPhone. This is the biggest negative. Starting, stopping and pausing is cumbersome as the phone is in a pocket and often in a protective bag. The GPS also churns through the battery, it’ll completely drain on a 3 hour ride.

Garmin Connect

I was wary of Garmin Connect. Their client side software sucks and I thought Connect would be not much more than a bullet point on Garmin product’s feature lists. My concern was unfounded, Connect is highly polished and functional. I’d go as far to say it’s one of the better web applications out there.

Positives

  • Good looking website that’s all round, easy to use and navigate.
  • Attractive, readable graphs.
  • Easy to get data from your device to Connect with their totally unobtrusive browser plugin.
  • Simple and useful reporting UI.
  • Being web based, easy to share.

Negatives

  • No map to graph interaction outside of a cumbersome Flash “player”.
  • No ability to layer graphs or compare rides.
  • Somewhat inflexible, you can’t enlarge the map or graphs.

Rubitrack

Rubitrack is well put together and organised, a major advantage. UI conventions are followed and everything works the way you expect it to. The source list works like a contextual bunch of smart folders and it’s my favourite way any of these applications organise rides. But while it does some things brilliantly, it does others poorly.

Positives

  • Well organised, Mac like UI.
  • Crystal clear map that visually represents zones.

Negatives

  • Crappy graphs in many ways. No interaction with the map, visually confusing and doesn’t display precise readings on hover. In practice, they’re static jpegs.
  • Forces you to save and maintain a library file which it doesn’t automatically load on next open. I hate this convention. When I’m using an application to interact with a “library” of something, I don’t want to manage afile, I don’t want to save or open, I want the data side of things to be invisible. I can’t think of any situation where I’d want to manage multiple Rubitrack libraries simultaneously.
  • The calendar view has potential but is poorly executed.

TrailRunner

For donationware TrailRunner is surprisingly good but tries to be too much at the expense of absolutely nailing what it already does well. In some areas it’s polished to perfection, in others it almost seems broken.

Positives

  • The best looking graphs of any of the applications listed here. Far and away the most well thought out and executed. Very easy to read layered graphs.
  • Ability to select a section of the graph which it will then display averages for and display that selection on the map. A brilliant idea that no other app has implemented let alone done so well.
  • Great looking maps and seamless graph + map integration.

Negatives

  • Disorganised UI. Sometimes I will get lost and don’t know how to get back to my list of rides. Labels are messy and unclear.
  • Some functions popup confusing windows which aren’t obvious how they integrate into the rest of the application.
  • Ugly, annoying copyright banner on the bottom of the map. I assume this is a requirement for map use without a commercial license but it’s distracting.
  • The same frustrating save file model as Rubitrack.

Ascent

Ascent is the Final Cut Pro of this category of applications. Raw UI with every kind of function at your disposal. The UI is just plain ugly and acts strangely, drop down buttons act like toggle switches and new windows are opened all over the place. It’s a powerful application but I can’t look past the unpolished UI.

Positives

  • The option to dim the map background results in the best looking maps out of any application here.
  • There’s a great map view that shows your zones along the route. I don’t understand why this isn’t the default.

Negatives

  • Map and graph interaction is only possible when you have the graph popup open even though a graph exists as part of the main window.
  • Again, Ascent forces you to manage library files! Why do they do this?! Could you imagine having to manually save your iTunes library every time you added a song to a playlist or change the smallest piece of meta data.
  • I simply can’t tolerate the interface and interaction quirks. There’s one text box which opens up a modal text box when it gains focus. Frustrating to use.

Garmin Training Center

Granted it’s a “beta” but Garmin Training Center is too bad to dwell on. Don’t waste your time.

Conclusion

There is definitely no clear winner. All the client side options have the requirement for saving library files which pisses me off so much it’s a deal breaker across the board. Although each of them have their own individual pitfalls outside of the file issue. For now I’m sticking with Garmin Connect, although it lacks some features, it makes up for it by doing what it does do well. The UI is polished and and great reporting tools which I appreciate.

I’ve looked into the files that my Garmin Edge collects and they’re nothing more than simple XML files which opens it up for me to play with this data myself. I had a great time creating the graph on ride.jimwhimpey.com with Raphael, so I’m looking forward to diving into my cycling data with Graphael and perhaps complimenting Garmin Connect with my own software.

Revisiting My Ideal House Plan

18 months ago I wrote about my ideal house. Back then I was still living in my large, suburban, family home, hadn’t yet graduated and didn’t have much of a job. The old plan looked like this:

Things couldn’t be much more different now. I have a job that pays well and I’ve lived 5 minutes outside the city in an apartment with my girlfriend for the better part of a year.

After 8 months living in an apartment my take on ideal living arrangements has changed — but not much. Most of the points I raised in my original post stand true now. Free standing houses beat apartments for windows and backyards, houses are usually much too large, one bathroom is enough, dining rooms are a waste of space, an outdoor area is important. I still agree with all these things and I think my original plan stands up well, I’d happily live alone in it today.

There was one glaring omission from the original plan that I mentioned but didn’t address, the complete lack of storage beyond a single wardrobe. I’m not one to needlessly keep much but often it’s unavoidable and it’s frustrating not having enough space or the right space kind to store things.

That’s the main issue I’ve tried to address in my revised plan but there’s lots of other stuff there too:

At first it looks much bigger, something I’m trying to avoid but note that I’ve included the patio/verandah area in this plan and as one floor is a basement, the overall footprint has only increased about 15%.

The biggest addition is the basement. You’d be hard pressed to find a house in Australia with a basement which is crazy, they’re a great, efficient use of space and could provide respite from the Summer heat. It addresses the lack of storage with the room on the left totally dedicated to it. I imagine lining those walls with shelves and cupboards, all removable to keep that room flexible.

The rest of the basement is multipurpose. It’s somewhat a typical garage space but I don’t own a car and don’t think cars belong inside anyway. Part laundry, part place to keep my bikes, bike paraphernalia, other training equipment and tools.

The door on the right wall of the basement is a typical garage roller door. Having to go to a separate garage or dragging my bike through the apartment sucks when I’m going in and out on bikes 4 times or more every day. I imagine a ramp driveway cut into the ground to get down there. It’s not space efficient but I don’t see another way. That and the space stealing need for stairs are the worst thing about the addition of a basement.

I’ve pulled the study into it’s own room. I have too much computer related stuff that’d clutter up the lounge room area. I also like the idea of totally concentrating on getting things done when in the office and using a laptop outside or on the couch for more “recreational” computing.

Basically it’s the same plan with additional storage and garage space.

Selling

I have zero concept of sentimental value and no hoarding instinct. I like to sell the things I don’t use any more. Recently I’ve been trying to sell an old Dell screen and a USB microphone.

I started at Gumtree (Australia’s Craigslist (although Craigslist does cover Australia (it’s just not very popular (which is vital to these sites usefulness)))) for two reasons.

It’s easier, list the item for free, interested parties inquire, if they want it they come and get it in exchange for cash. Very little time investment and minimal fucking around. I also like the concept of real life community a site like Gumtree fosters. More people should look locally for more things.

Both items were on Gumtree for a month with nothing but bullshit, time wasting, substance-less enquiries. I must have had 20 “is it still for sale?” emails. Of course it’s still for sale, that’s why it’s still listed under the fucking For Sale section of Gumtree. It’s like going to a supermarket and asking if those bananas are for sale.

I told each person that yes, it’s still for sale with not a single follow up. It’s like they were only curious if it was still for sale, not at all interested in actually buying anything.

There were a number of people asking where they could pick it up, I told them, they didn’t follow up. One guy arranged to meet me and never showed, when he finally answered my call half an hour after we were supposed to meet he made up some blubbering excuse and tried to organise another meet up. No.

Each week I’d lower the price — well below offers that’d previously been made with no follow up — there was no change in the response.

In an ideal world Gumtree is the place for getting rid of your stuff to people genuinely interested. In reality it’s 99% tyre kickers more interested in your time than what you’ve got to sell.

Fed up, I list them both on Ebay with a opening price and offer nationwide shipping. Bidding activity is strong, they sell at a price I’m happy with, the buyer pays immediately, the next day I ship them both at the expense of the buyer.

It cost me $8 and the hassle of shipping but was 100 times easier than entertaining the deluge of bullshit Gumtree inquiries.

The iMac versus the Mac Pro

Editor’s note: This post includes an embedded table which lacks styles when viewed in the dashboard but which I’ve meticulously styled for the post when viewed on the website, I suggest you view it there.

I’m obsessed with the ideal setup. I try to milk the best out of the equipment I have and think a lot about how it could be improved as my situation changes and new technology is released.

I’ve been more than satisfied with my 24” iMac at home for a long time but I feel like I’ve outgrown it. The Mac Pro was the next, extremely expensive, logical step. Until the 27” iMac arrived:

 
Processor 2.66Ghz Quad Core Intel Core i5 2.66GHz Quad Core Intel Xeon Nehalem
Memory 4GB 3GB
Hard Disk 1TB 640GB
Video Card ATI Radeon HD 4850 graphics with 512MB NVIDIA GeForce GT 120 with 512MB
Screen 27” LED backlit display at 2560 x 1440 (16:9) None
Price AU$2,599 AU$4,499

Equivalent CPU power and (I guess) video cards, the iMac wins on RAM and hard disk space which are both cheap enough to be negligible. So as far as computer specifications go, they’re very similar, if not totally equivalent out of the box.

The iMac comes with a gorgeous, 27” LED backlit screen the likes of which you can’t even buy individually. The Mac Pro comes with no screen. The Mac Pro is expandable with PCI cards and hard disk slots. The cost of that expandability is $1900 and the best computer screen available.

For many the lack of expandability is a deal breaker. If expandability is not essential, the iMac is an easy choice and I imagine many Mac Pro users will defect to the iMac come upgrade time.

Expandability is not essential for me but it’s desirable. Evidently Apple is able to sell a computer with the power of the Mac Pro for much less than $4,499. I wish they’d put it in the Mac Pro’s expandable case and sell it at or below the iMac’s fantastic price.
