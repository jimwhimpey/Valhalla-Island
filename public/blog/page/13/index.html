What is Open?

Yesterday’s post on Flash’s claims of openness was linked to on Daring Fireball (and later followed up) and resulted in a lot of emails telling me how wrong I am.

Admittedly, I was wrong and posted an amendment. In Gruber’s followup he wrote:

I still say Whimpey’s conclusion stands — it’s not the tools that make Flash a closed platform, it’s that the runtime, Flash Player, is completely controlled and defined by Adobe. Adobe’s argument is that Flash is an open part of the web but that there just happens to exist only one implementation, their own closed-source Flash Player.

Adobe has published the SWF file format specification which theoretically allows anyone to build authoring tools and interpreters for Flash content. In practice the number one open source Flash player (or at least the only one with any presence online) barely supports anything. From the gnash website:

[…] and supports most SWF v7 features and some SWF v8 and v9

SWF is currently at version 10. The page proudly listing open source and free development tools has a section on players:

While the specs for creating SWF files are freely available, it’s not currently possible to create fully featured Flash Players as Open Source. The main reason is that Adobe’s implementation of the Flash Player makes use of technology licensed from several companies.

So my original point stands:

Adobe: not open, claim to be.

Apple: not open, don’t claim to be, contribute heavily to that which is truly open.

Adobe®: We Love Choice

Adobe®: We Love Choice

The $600 Flash authoring tool is the only way to produce Flash applications.

The free Mac OS X Developer tools (that require an $800+ Mac computer) are the only way to produce iPhone and iPad applications.

Both systems are as closed as each other.

Adobe claims Flash is open.

Apple makes no such claims. If you want to make iPhone or iPad apps you use Mac OS and Xcode, take it or leave it.

If you choose to leave it the alternative is HTML. A truly open platform for which authoring is as simple as editing text. There’s many HTML rendering engine implementations, the best few are totally open source with Apple being the major contributor to the best one, WebKit.

Adobe: not open, claim to be.

Apple: not open, don’t claim to be, contribute heavily to that which is truly open.

Update

As many Daring Fireball readers have pointed out to me via email, I was wrong about Flash (the authoring tool) being the only way to publish SWF files.

SWF is a standard that Adobe has published information on how to produce. However, Flash (the player) is the only appication that can interpret SWF files and my closing point stands.

Developer Designer

I agree with Julio Ody in that design competency is hugely advantageous for developers but disagree on the tools he first suggests:

Stop believing Photoshop, Pixelmator, Illustrator, or anything else is too hard.

They are too hard, too inefficient, too poorly suited to designing web UIs, too expensive and simply unnecessary. It’s followed by much better advice:

Still don’t want to fork the cash for these suites, or you consider yourself really incapable of learning any of them? Design straight in HTML and CSS.

Anything you can’t do in HTML and CSS is more design than any developer should be concerning themselves with. Besides, it’s faster, less fiddly, more precise, easily scriptable, repeatable, you’ll always have real content to work with and obviously, it’ll always look exactly like the end result. Starting your design in HTML and CSS (or better, HAML and SASS) is simply better.

This blog, Campus Notes, A Solid Base and Darkroom were all designed with minimal or (more commonly) no contact at all with a graphics editor. While none of them are visually stunning, that’s not the goal. The goal is not letting poor design get in the way and not having to rely on a designer to prevent it.

Three years ago I wrote a post about simple things you can do to help your design suck less. All of my suggestions still stand today and can be implemented more efficiently without thousands of dollars worth of bloated design software.
